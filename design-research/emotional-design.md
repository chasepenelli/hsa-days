# Emotional Design Principles

How to handle grief, health, and emotional content with grace in a digital interface.

---

## Three Pillars (from Smashing Magazine's Empathy-Centred UX framework)

### 1. Onboarding as a Supportive First Conversation
Not a signup form. The first interaction should feel like someone saying "I'm here." Language matters more than layout in this moment.

### 2. Interface Design for a Brain in Distress
Low stimulus, familiar, no jarring elements. People in crisis have reduced cognitive bandwidth — every unnecessary decision or visual distraction is a cost.

### 3. Retention Through Trust, Not Pressure
Deepen trust rather than pressure users. No streaks, no guilt, no "you missed a day!" notifications. Show up because it feels good, not because you'll lose something.

---

## Specific Principles

### Language & Framing
- **Validation first**: "What you're feeling is normal" before any guidance or action
- **Daily content as ritual, not obligation**: The language around showing up matters enormously
- **Progress framing**: "You showed up for N days" not "N/30 complete" — the first celebrates presence, the second implies a quota
- **Non-clinical vocabulary**: "reflection" not "assessment", "journal" not "log", "companion" not "tool"

### Visual & Interaction Design
- **Avoid sudden animations** or jarring bright alerts — they trigger stress responses in distressed users
- **Subtle, predictable micro-interactions** rather than performative ones
- **Color palette should be grounding**, not stimulating — earthy, non-neon (the existing palette is correct)
- **Generous whitespace** is not wasted space — it's emotional safety
- **No countdown timers** or urgency signals — these amplify anxiety

### Content Delivery
- Morning delivery is correct — it frames the day gently
- Each day should feel self-contained — not a cliffhanger, not "to be continued"
- Include a clear "you can come back to this anytime" signal
- Never make the user feel behind

---

## Anti-Patterns to Avoid

| Pattern | Why It's Harmful |
|---------|-----------------|
| Streak counters | Creates guilt and obligation, not healing |
| "You missed Day 5!" notifications | Shame-based engagement |
| Before/after comparisons | Implies grief has a "correct" trajectory |
| Clinical scale ratings (1-10) | Reduces complex emotions to numbers |
| Forced sharing | Grief is private until the person chooses otherwise |
| Time-limited access | Creates urgency in a context that needs patience |
| Pop-ups or modals on emotional content | Jarring interruption during vulnerable moments |

---

## What Good Looks Like

The best emotional design is invisible. The user should feel:
- "This understands me" (validation)
- "I can breathe here" (whitespace, pace)
- "This is mine" (privacy, no judgment)
- "I'm not alone" (community, shared experience)
- "I can do this" (manageable daily steps)

The interface should feel like a warm, quiet room — not a hospital, not a classroom, not a social media feed.
